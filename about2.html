Mission

Hurricane Coast exists to publish practical, household-level preparedness guidance for hurricane-exposed communities. The goal is not to cover every part of disaster policy. The goal is to make early preparation easier, clearer, and more actionable for ordinary readers.

Every page should answer one or both of these questions: what should a household do now, and why does that recommendation matter before a storm is imminent?

Editorial scope

The site focuses on preparedness kits, planning logic, printable checklists, and geography-specific guidance for coasts where hurricane planning materially changes. It is intentionally narrower than a general emergency news site and intentionally more explanatory than a one-page government checklist.

The project is built for people trying to prepare a household, not for readers looking for product reviews, storm entertainment, or alarmist content. That narrow scope is a feature, not a limitation.

Source policy

Where official guidance exists, the site should point to it directly. FEMA, the National Hurricane Center, local emergency management offices, the Red Cross, CDC, and similar authorities are referenced when they are the correct source of record.

The value of the site is not pretending to replace those agencies. The value is organizing, translating, and contextualizing their guidance for a reader who needs something clearer and more practical than a raw PDF or advisory page.

Limits and claims

This project does not provide medical advice, structural engineering advice, or storm forecasting. It does not claim professional emergency management authority. When a topic exceeds the site’s expertise, the page should say so plainly.

That boundary matters. Preparedness content is most trustworthy when it is explicit about what it can help with and what it cannot.

Maintenance standard

Pages are reviewed before June 1, the start of hurricane season, and revised after major storms, outdated references, or reporting gaps. Broken links, stale assumptions, or ambiguous recommendations are treated as maintenance problems rather than cosmetic problems.

The standard is not perfection. The standard is useful, current, honest guidance that can withstand scrutiny and revision.

Support this work

The site runs without ads or affiliate revenue. If the material has saved you time or helped your household prepare, a tip helps fund research, revisions, and hosting while keeping the project independent.
