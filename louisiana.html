Preparing for hurricanes in Louisiana

Louisiana faces hurricane risk that is shaped by geography unlike anywhere else in the US. The Mississippi River delta is slowly sinking. Coastal wetlands that once absorbed storm surge have been eroding for decades. The result is that hurricane impact in Louisiana — even from storms that make landfall elsewhere — can be severe.

What makes hurricanes here different

Louisiana's vulnerability is a combination of geography, geology, and infrastructure.

  • Land subsidence means parts of coastal Louisiana are at or below sea level — not because of flooding, but as a baseline. Storm surge in these areas doesn't just reach the coast; it arrives where the coast used to be.
  • Coastal wetland loss has dramatically reduced Louisiana's natural storm buffer. Wetlands slow surge and absorb rainfall. Their reduction is well-documented; the implications for storm surge penetration are significant.
  • Slow-moving storms have historically caused some of Louisiana's most damaging flooding — slow movement means more rainfall accumulation and longer surge exposure.
  • The Mississippi River adds complexity: flooding from rivers and flooding from surge can interact, creating compound flood events.
  • Chemical facilities along the Mississippi River corridor mean that industrial hazards during a storm are a real consideration for some areas of the state.

Regional supply additions for Louisiana

Louisiana-specific conditions — extreme summer humidity, mosquito pressure, potential chemical exposure concerns in the corridor areas, and the possibility of prolonged post-storm flooding — add items the standard kit doesn't cover.

  • Insect repellent (DEET-based) — mosquito pressure after flooding is severe; mosquito-borne illness rises after storms
  • Extra water beyond baseline — humidity and heat increase water needs significantly during power outages
  • N95 respirators — mold grows within 24–48 hours in Louisiana's humid climate after flooding; this is not optional
  • Waterproof bags for everything — the probability of flooding is high enough that every item should be treated as water-exposed
  • A battery-powered fan — summer power outages in Louisiana carry heat risk comparable to Texas and Florida

Official sources to bookmark now

The organizations below are the authoritative sources for evacuation orders, shelter locations, and storm-specific guidance. Bookmark these before the season — not when a storm is approaching, when traffic on these sites is enormous.

  • Louisiana Governor's Office of Homeland Security and Emergency Preparedness — official state emergency management
  • National Hurricane Center — track forecasts and warnings
  • Louisiana 511 — real-time road conditions
  • Louisiana 211 — local assistance
  • NOLA Ready (New Orleans) — city-specific emergency management
  • Your parish emergency management office — search "[your parish] Louisiana emergency preparedness" for local orders and shelters
  • FEMA / ready.gov — federal preparedness resources

Evacuation routes and shelter locations change with each event. Always confirm with your parish emergency management office.

Historical context

Louisiana has been shaped by hurricanes as much as by any other force. The 2005 hurricane season — Katrina and Rita within weeks of each other — exposed the interaction of storm surge, levee failure, and inadequate evacuation infrastructure in ways that led to substantial reform of federal, state, and local emergency management.

The Louisiana State University AgCenter's documentation of post-Katrina recovery, and the Army Corps of Engineers' post-Katrina flood protection improvements, represent significant institutional learning. More recent storms including Ida in 2021 tested the rebuilt systems. The state's emergency management infrastructure is more capable now than it was in 2005; it is also working in an environment of ongoing coastal land loss that continues to increase vulnerability.
