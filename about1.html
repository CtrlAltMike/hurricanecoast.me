What this project is

Hurricane Supply List is an independent preparedness site for people who want clear household guidance before a storm is on the map. It is not a news product, and it is not a gear catalog. It is a working library of kit guides, coastal planning pages, and printable checklists meant to help a household make calmer decisions earlier.

I built it because there is a large gap between official emergency information, which is often correct but hard to absorb, and commercial preparedness content, which is often easy to read but financially motivated. This site tries to do the middle thing well: plain language, no commissions, and enough reasoning that a reader can understand why a recommendation belongs on the list.

Who I am

I'm Mike. I'm not a meteorologist, emergency manager, or disaster response professional. I'm someone who lives with real disaster risk and has spent a long time learning what useful preparedness advice looks like when it has to work inside an actual household.

I also built cascadia.me for Pacific Northwest earthquake readiness. That project shaped the standard I use here too: make the guidance calm, specific, and practical enough that someone would actually send it to a spouse, parent, neighbor, or friend.

How I decide what belongs here

I bias toward boring recommendations that survive real-world constraints. If a suggestion depends on expensive gear, perfect discipline, or a shopping list that only works for one kind of household, it usually does not belong. I care more about what a family will realistically do on a Tuesday night than what looks comprehensive in a roundup article.

The question behind most pages is simple: if a storm were forming this week, would this advice reduce confusion, save time, or keep a household from forgetting something important? If not, it does not earn space.

Where I draw the line

I do not forecast storms, offer medical advice, or make structural claims about homes and buildings. When official agencies are the right authority, I point readers there. When a page reflects my own synthesis or judgment, I try to make that obvious.

The site is meant to complement local emergency management, not replace it. The best preparedness plan is still the one built around your own geography, evacuation routes, health needs, and household realities.

How I keep it current

I review the site before hurricane season begins on June 1 and again after major storms or policy changes that expose a bad assumption, a broken link, or a missing detail. I would rather revise quickly than let old copy sit there sounding confident.

If you spot something outdated, unclear, or wrong, I want to hear about it. A preparedness site only deserves trust if it is willing to correct itself.
